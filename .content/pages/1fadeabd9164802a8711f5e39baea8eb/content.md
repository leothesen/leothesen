I entered the MUT 100km race a while back, I’m not entirely sure why. I mean it was something that I wanted to do, and I was excited by it, but I didn’t really have a concise and clear reason as to why I entered. 
<empty-block/>
It’s also the thing that many people asked when they found out that I was going to do it. And “to finish” really isn’t an adequate response. This is a little piece of writing about why I would do something like this, and why you could too. Not in a motivational, "Just Do It” - Shia Labeouf kind of way, but trying to shed some light and clarity on running 100km as a normal human with a life outside of running. 
<empty-block/>
Let’s take it right back: I started running around April 2021. I had run quite a lot during School days due to hockey - but after matriculating in 2012 I hardly ran, and was rather dismissive of it. I got back into it after hiking the Otter Trail and wanting to spend more time in the mountains, and quickly fell in love with trail running around Cape Town. 
<empty-block/>
Look - yes I’m waffling here, but I feel that it’s good context. Stay with me here, it’s going somewhere. Next comes Damo, a dear friend is the next important character to introduce here.
<empty-block/>
So there’s two ways to run an ultra marathon:
1. A-Type science: measure everything. Calculate your sweat rates to know how much electrolytes to drink per hour, VO2 max, resting heart rates, Zone 2, 75km weeks, HRV monitoring, sleep tracking, lactate thresholds and so on and so forth. 
2. Damo’s way: love being in the mountains, always have fun, don’t run every day, take in the views, use you mind more than your body, everything is an adventure, laugh and enjoy the experience. 
<empty-block/>
Yes, if you want to win races - option 1 is probably what you’re going to choose. Yet I don’t think that most people want to win races, they just want to move with ease through beautiful mountains for a long time. And they want to have a life that contains other joys, people, activities and work.

Since then he led me on other fastpacking and running trips around the country. Then I planned a few. My natural A-Type predisposition balanced and tamed by his adventurous and intuitive way of doing things. Something that I believe was the key to the MUT 100k.
<empty-block/>
Damo has done a number of 100 milers, and supporting him through UTCT (for which he hardly trained in most people’s eyes) I could see how it was his mind that kept him going. And it got me thinking, maybe ultra marathons can basically just be a mental game?

I had a fairly strong running base coming into 2025: had run a handful of marathons, the UTCT 55km and UTD 64km. For UTD I was deep into a year long ITB struggle and just power hiked the whole thing. Something that I think everyone should try - you’d be surprised how fast you can go if you consistently fast hike at less than 12 minutes/km (which is the cutoff for most ultras).

I did some strength training: with kettle bells at home for 10 minutes after a run, and then went to the gym about 7 times in the last two months. This helped so much, I feel like you get more bang for your buck with strength training than you think. 
<empty-block/>
All in all - I didn’t train incredibly hard. I just tried to live my life, do an activity every day. Surf, hike, socialise, work, sleep well. Basically, just an active life. 
<empty-block/>
I listened to a podcast called [Science of Ultra](https://open.spotify.com/show/1F1HpfBJwgKExXQdyE7JbC), which had some nuggets of wisdom in there. Much of it is A-Type anxiety inducing science from coaches, but the episodes that Shawn just monologs about his Buddhist-esque running philosophy is truly gold. 
<empty-block/>
Mentally there was training too. I can’t underestimate meditation enough when it comes to training the mind. The [Waking Up](https://www.wakingup.com/) app really is a fantastic resource here. I ended my job at the end of April and was stress free in May, which meant that I was going into the race calm. The week before I went up to my parents in Knysna and slept for 10 - 12 hours a night, probably something that did more good than running 60km per week could ever do.

I chatted with a few people running the marathon, one stands out: after following closely behind one guy for some time I asked how he was doing. The noise that came out his mouth resembled a monster from the depths of hell. A rasping low grumble which must have burnt his infected throat with every syllable. Keeping his sentences brief he told me that we was very sick but having a great time. I wished him health and quickly overtook him for fear of infection. 
<empty-block/>
I follow friendly Amanda down the endless gradual descent from Dizzy Heights. The path feels like a bed of moss, springy underfoot. We chat about everything from running to haircuts. With a wave she runs off. We merge with another group and excitedly mutter short phrases, I get carried along at a fast pace down the beautiful trail by their enthusiasm. I can clearly remember their shoes and calves, but probably didn’t look at any of their faces - funny, that. 
<empty-block/>
The Tierkop aid station appears and I can hear the music. I’m 43km in. When I arrive there must have been about forty people under the stretch tent and I immediately feel like I’m at a festival. One of the aid station workers comes up to me and offers me a chair promising to come back with my drop bag. When she returns she squats downs and introduces herself as Cindy, then promptly takes care of my every need.

My mind went pretty quiet at this point. Egoic thoughts almost entirely stopped. Physical sensations, my visual field and sounds were pretty much my entire experience. Presence felt effortless and observing everything without judgement was the status quo. There was a little pain in my right knee from the oncoming ITB - but to be honest pain is something that you sign up for with these long runs. It was easy to just watch the pain move and change over time. I could adjust my gait to the point where it would disappear, coming back again on steeper descents.  
<empty-block/>
I suspect that for many people, pain is binary: you’re either in pain or not in pain. And then if you’re in pain things are wrong and you must stop, all the alarm bells comes on in the mind and the negative thoughts follow closely behind. I try to view pain as more of a spectrum. Like the temperature gauge in a car - if you follow my analogy - a cold car is no pain, and redlining hot is the most pain you could experience. You drive a car, happily, with the temperature gauge at like 20%. If you hit 100% you should stop driving. The same goes for your body, you can run just fine with a bit of pain. If you view any pain in the same way you view an overheating car, then you’re not ever going to run. Just monitor the pain, watch it change and move. If it’s located in one specific area and gets more and more intense over time, then something is probably wrong. But if it travels around an area and stays manageable - that’s pretty normal. Just be aware of it and be kind to it.

I arrive at the next aid station Groeneweide. 72km in and the end is suddenly in sight, it’s just a weekend run away now and at this rate I’m going to finish around midnight. Or so I think. It’s a cute aid station, nestled in the forest with a simple stretch tent, a few 4x4s, a speaker playing golden oldies and a few camp chairs around a table. The night is warm and one of the volunteers comes up to greet me. 
<empty-block/>
I ask him to do two things:
1. Force feed me
2. Kick me out after 20 minutes
<empty-block/>
I choose the most uncomfortable chair (which will make leaving easier) and have a cup of coffee. There are five other people at the aid station - who I was with in the beginning of the race - a crew of four British doctors working at the George hospital and two South African colleagues. They’re all in their thirties and full of excitement and banter. They talk of the “triple threat” which will get them through what is to come: a monster energy that they were given at Dikkop, a potato and a Panado. I’m offered a sip of Monster on account of it being my birthday, it tastes like a mango vape. They ask me if I’m ready for the climb to come - I haven’t really studied the route profile and say I prefer not to know. They wish me luck and set off ahead of me. 
<empty-block/>

I get kicked out of Groeneweide after twenty minutes and head off into the night, spirits high. It’s 13km to Tierkop and I’m so excited to see Cindy again. Thirteen kilometres doesn’t feel too long, I put the playlist on and pick up the pace. 
<empty-block/>
What proceeds is the hardest 10km I’ve ever gone through. We start with the world’s longest jeep track ascent which goes up \~450m in 5km, it winds its way into the night - the head torches of the British crew always just in the distance. Climbing this was fine, but it took it out of me. 
<empty-block/>
As I hit the summit the route goes onto a single track path, although I’m not sure I can call it a path. Loose sand and rock aimed directly down and it drops the 450m in 1.5km. The descent hurts, the ITB comes back strong and I awkwardly slide and step my way down. I take a fall and slide a meter or so, which brings on a rush of adrenalin which actually does wonders for my concentration and I make it to the bottom unscathed. 
<empty-block/>
Using ropes to get down the last few meters we hit Kaaimans river. Here too are ropes to guide you across the slippery rocks, by some miracle I get across with dry shoes. The ropes continue up the near vertical slope on the other side of the river and begins the most challenging climb of the race (and my life). It’s 450m up again in 1km. A ladder would be helpful here. I’ve done 80km and over 3000m of vertical climbing and my body is tired. 
<empty-block/>
The poles save me up this hill. I plant them as far ahead as possible, put my body weight on them and take a few small steps with the feet to bring my body slowly. Then repeat, for what feels like forever. My mind doesn’t turn negative here, it actually just feels comical how hard it is. I take regular breaks, leaning against my poles and looking back at the descent into Kaaimans on the other side of the valley. There are several lights from other runners coming down, if only they knew what was in store for them.

	# What I learned
:::
<empty-block/>
This is mostly as a reference for myself, the next time I decide to do something like this - but maybe someone else will find it helpful:
<empty-block/>
::: callout {icon="🥘" color="blue_bg"}
	**Food**
	- Whole, real food over gel ever time. Eat as much as you can at each aid station
	- Even though you’re craving sugar, salty food is what your body needs (although a code coke does go down well when it’s hot - write it off against needing caffeine)
	- A tailwind sachet in 1.5L of water ever 15km keeps the mind and body incredibly sharp
:::
::: callout {icon="🥤" color="purple_bg"}
	**Drink**
	- Revive is the electrolyte that agrees best with me. Put one in each soft flask
	- Coffee is great, just have it black
:::
::: callout {icon="🚶‍♀️" color="green_bg"}
	**Pace**
	- When running: run as slowly as possible
	- When hiking: hike as quickly as possible
	- I’m never going to race these things, so power hiking is actually a secret weapon. Try start doing it as early into the race as possible - by the end everyone near you is going to be walking like drunk babies anyway, so keep some gas in the tank to finish strong
:::
::: callout {icon="🏋️‍♀️" color="pink_bg"}
	**Training**
	- You can run 100km with not much more than 20km per week training - don’t give up your life for these events
	- Weight training is vital - get into the habit of doing it after ever run and at least a few times a week
	- Stretching/yoga - I wish I had done more
	- Train you mind more than your body - it’s the thing that will ultimately get you through and allow you to enjoy the experience
	- Just do something active every day - it doesn’t have to be running
	- Choose the stairs over lifts
	- Resting and sleeping the week before probably does more good than running ever would (you can’t be unemployed during each ultra, but try prioritise it sleep the week before the next one)

	- Use poles - four legs are better than two

::: callout {icon="❓" color="orange_bg"}
	# Why I did it
	And why I would do another again
:::
First and foremost it is a privilege to be able to do this. To have a body able to run and walk. To be in the mountains. To have a mind that views doing these things as a peak experience in life. I don’t take it for granted, and this is actually wisdom imparted on Damo by Brundle, then passed on to me in the phone call we had that night. It’s a huge privilege that I won’t take for granted and will honour and respect. 
<empty-block/>
To be able to spend the whole day in the mountains, on a beautiful trail is something that I love. And to do this for longer than I’ve done in the past was a core reason for signing up to this distance. And it might be the reason I end up doing something longer in the future.
<empty-block/>
It’s physical meditation. To live purely in experience, in your body for an extended period of time. To view everything simply as experience, not judging things as good or bad, but just watching it go by. 
<empty-block/>
The beautiful mountains.
