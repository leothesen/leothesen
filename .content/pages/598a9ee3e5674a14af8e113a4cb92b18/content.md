The two dogs haven’t shown the slightest hint of weakness yet. They refuse our water, opting only to drink from stagnant puddles mostly consisting of cow shit. They run without stop, I imagine that they have easily covered 35km at this point with all of their exploratory detours.
<empty-block/>
A rocky cliff requires some climbing to descend to a long beach that leads us to the lodge. Damo and I get down, Jeanine follows us with some difficulty and the three of us watch as Paul begins to get anxious about being left behind. We slowly walk away and he decides to throw caution to the wind and clumsily fall down the rocks to join us.
<empty-block/>
I’ve known that we were going to pick up some dogs at some point. Many people have told me that it is a bad idea as the roaming dogs of the Transkei are known to occasionally kill sheep to which the Shepard’s reclaim their lost livestock with a currency of R1000 per sheep. Paul and Jeanine have been docile and friendly - I don’t have too many concerns. 
<empty-block/>
On the beach Paul’s demeanour suddenly changes. He’s noticeably agitated and begins showing signs of aggression towards Jeanine. He nips at our ankles and barks incessantly. Damo and I find it rather strange and comedic. Then suddenly he looks us right in the eye, growls slightly, then turns and sprints back in the direction that he came from. 
<empty-block/>
A few hundred meters away he stops, barks and circles incessantly. His mind is once again made up - he sprints off into the golden light, not to be seen again. Jeanine is relatively unfazed by this and follows at our side. I wonder how often these dogs roam the coast, using hikers as busses from backpackers to backpackers. 
<empty-block/>
At the foot of the lodge is a small river. Shoes come off and we cross. Jeanine is unsure for a while then follows us. We see Fred up in the lodge with another man by his side, standing behind a large window.
<empty-block/>
The lodge sits on the bank of a beautiful river and grassy headland. It’s truly beautiful and isolated. Fred welcomes us with two beers and while the figure watches ominously behind the glass. As we enter he introduces himself as Andrew and asks questions directly without much emotion or concern. He says that the lodge is full tonight but we can camp outside for R150 each.
<empty-block/>
We order dinner at the lodge and go for a shower. Fred is so excited to show us how it works. Follow me he says:
1. Grab a small bottle of paraffin 
2. Unroll a toilet roll five times
3. Pour the paraffin in this vessel and place it at the bottom of this large metal cylinder
4. Place the toilet paper on top
5. Light the paraffine
6. Open the tap that sends water through the jet of flames 
7. Enjoy
It’s a pretty incredible system and offers a warm shower for nearly ten minutes. Apparently it’s a mission to clean though…
<empty-block/>
Jeanine sits patiently outside and as a tour bus pulls in. Around fifteen American tourists throng around us - talking intently in groups. The three of us sit outside and reflect on the day, we play rose (a highlight), rose, thorn (a lowlight) and bud (something that you’re excited for):
Roses:
- The last beautiful river crossing
- Walking over an enormous hill and getting our first glimpse of a magical bay called “Secret Beach”
- Walking into the sunset with Paul and Jeanine
- The beautiful coastline dotted with fishermen and children
<empty-block/>
Thorns:
- Being slightly on guard and anxious at hole in the wall. The rumours of crime had prevented us completely relaxing and enjoying the space.
<empty-block/>
Buds:
- Looking forward to tomorrow’s nicotine withdrawal as our tobacco has run out
- The sunrise start that we’ve decided to do seeing that tomorrow is going to be really hot.
<empty-block/>
A bon fire is started and the Americans begin to show interest in us. A girl named Elly sits with us and just begins talking absolute nonsense. She makes up quite a few elaborate lies that we’re from the states and and convinces a few other more gullible travellers that we know each other. 
<empty-block/>
Andrew tells us how he’s here to consult and help the backpackers recover after the pandemic. He’s like a pessimistic [Murray Hewitt](https://flightoftheconchords.fandom.com/wiki/Murray_Hewitt) but definitely makes us feel quite uncomfortable. Damo has picked up on the way that he’s treating the staff and has heard some disgruntled comments coming from the kitchen.
<empty-block/>
Dinner is served - mutton curry - we dish up gigantic portions and it’s delicious. Andrew asks some of the staff to being drumming. The Americans add flavour to it with some drinking games. After eating a few kilograms of food we decide to set up camp. Surveying the cold wind and grass outside does not bode well for camping so we decide to occupy the inside common room, pulling a partition closed as a sort of door. 
<empty-block/>
The drumming and drinking continues outside for many ours. We drift in and our of sleep. I hear the staff conspiring some sort of mutiny against Andrew in angry tones. I begin to feel sorry for him - he definitely has his heart in the right place, it’s just that his execution was performed without much tact or regard for the way that the local staff have been running the place up to now.
<empty-block/>
Suddenly Andrew wakes us - rather perturbed that we’re sleeping in the common room. We justify it with the fact that it’s simply too cold and windy outside. Damo comments on the fact that we would be paying R150 to wild camp and Andrew is immediately defensive and rather rude. The feeling of sympathy for Andrew fades quite quickly. 
<empty-block/>
The drum circle continues long into the night. Someone begins either washing dishes or throwing them around at 1am. Fred politely asks him to stop. All in all we have had an incredibly strange, uncomfortable and rather abrasive experience at Bulungula.
